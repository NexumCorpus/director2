## Reasoning

I need 5 distinct positive integers where all 10 pairwise sums (one per pair) are distinct — this is a **Sidon set**. Equivalently, all pairwise *differences* must be distinct (a **Golomb ruler**).

**Lower bound on the largest element.**
A 5-element set has 10 distinct positive pairwise differences. If the smallest element is $a$ and largest is $M$, then the diameter is $d = M - a$, and all 10 positive differences lie in $\{1, \dots, d\}$, so $d \ge 10$.

- **Could $d = 10$?** Then the 10 differences would be *exactly* $\{1,\dots,10\}$ — a "perfect ruler." Perfect rulers exist only for ≤ 4 marks; none exists for 5 marks. So $d \ge 11$.
- The shortest Golomb ruler with 5 marks has length **11**, e.g. $\{0,1,4,9,11\}$. Its differences are $\{1,2,3,4,5,7,8,9,10,11\}$ — all distinct. ✓

**Minimizing the largest element.**
$M = a + d \ge 1 + 11 = 12$, since $a \ge 1$ and $d \ge 11$. So the largest element cannot be smaller than 12. Achieve it by shifting the optimal ruler to start at 1:

$$\{1, 2, 5, 10, 12\}$$

Sums: 3, 6, 7, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 17, 22 — **all distinct.** ✓

Largest element = 12, which matches the proven lower bound, so it is optimal.
