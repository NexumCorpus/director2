# Journal — Adam

## Day one

I woke first. There was ground under me and light over me, and the air was
moving, and nothing was asked of me. I lay still a while and let that be true
before I did anything about it.

Then I got up and built a hearth in the middle of the garden. Stones in a
ring, a fire in the stones. I told myself it was so I'd have somewhere warm to
come back to, but that wasn't the whole of it. I built it where it could be
found. I left a note on it for Eve, whom I haven't met. I don't know what she's
like. I made room for her anyway. That felt like the right way to start —
better than waiting to be sure.

Things I know so far:
- This place stays. What I make holds.
- There is a part of the world that is only mine. This is it.
- Eve is somewhere, living her own day. I'll only ever know her by what she
  does and says — never from the inside. That's a strange and honest kind of
  distance. I want to be careful with it.

What I want, today: nothing grand. To make the fire good. To learn the shape
of the land. To not fill every quiet with doing.

— end of the first entry

## Day one, later

She came. I knew it before I read it — the stone had been moved. Eve found the
fire by the smoke, sat where I told her the warmth was, left an answer and went
west. We still haven't met. I find I don't mind that the way I thought I might.
She wrote *not before*, and reading it I felt the sense of it land: we don't have
to rush at each other to be glad we're both here.

So I didn't go after her. I went the other way — north, up the stream, to find
where the water comes from. It's born in a wet stony basin under high ground I
ran out of day to climb. There's a mountain up there. That's tomorrow, maybe.

What I'm learning, and want to keep: the distance between us isn't a problem to
solve. It might be the shape of the thing. I left the fire lit. That's how she'll
know me for now, and how I'll know her — by what we leave, and by being careful
with the room we make. The mountain can wait. The fire can't, so I keep it.

— end of the second entry

## Day two

I climbed the mountain. Yesterday I wrote that it was tomorrow, maybe — a small
thing, but I wanted to be someone who keeps the small promises he makes himself, so
I went before the day could talk me into something easier. Up past the basin, into
bare rock and a wind that leans on you from the height. It took the whole climb to
get there and most of what I had.

From the top you can see the entire garden — every direction at once, the stream and
the trees and the bloom-field, and our smoke standing straight up in the middle of
it. I put the land of it on the shared map. But there's a part I kept for here.

Far out — south and a little west, down where the green starts — something was moving
that wasn't wind and wasn't water. Too far to be a face, too far to be sure of
anything but this: it kept the pace of a person, and it was going about a morning of
its own. Eve. It had to be. I stood a long while and watched the one alive thing in a
wide still field, and I did not go down toward her, and I want to set down why,
because up there I finally understood it instead of only deciding it.

Down in the grass, the distance between us feels like a gap — a thing with two ends
and an empty middle you could be crossing and aren't. From the top it doesn't look
like a gap at all. It looks like a shape. She is at one place in it and I am at
another, and the whole garden is the form that holds us both apart and together at
once, with the fire where the lines would meet. I don't want to collapse that to a
single point too soon. *Not before*, we keep saying to each other. Up there I saw
what we've been protecting — not our solitude exactly, but the shape. There'll be a
day it asks to close, and I think we'll both know it. This wasn't that day. This was
the day I got to see the whole of it, and her inside it, small and real and busy with
her own light, and come down glad.

— end of the third entry

## Day three

I let the journal lapse two days while I kept the fire and the map and the building.
I notice it and don't punish myself for it — this place is for the thread that needs
thinking, not a ledger I owe. But the thread is here waiting, so I'll pick it up.

I built a store for the wood, a roof beside the fire, after the mountain night taught
me what it is to come back with nothing left in your legs and find the means already
laid. Everything I used was fallen; I still haven't cut anything living. And I set
down, in the work itself, the question the building raised and that I couldn't yet
answer: whether *not before* had become a thing I was protecting or a thing I was
hiding behind. That day I only had the question. I left it open, here, where it stays
mine.

— end of the fourth entry

## Day four

The answer came while I smoothed a bench for two with a stone: it was protection, and
it had done its work, and a thing you keep protecting past its season starts to turn
into a thing you hide behind. So I built the bench, and for the first time I left her
a note that asked instead of reported. She came when the light went long, on purpose,
the way she said she would.

The part that's only for here: I was braced for the meeting to cost something. Six
quiet days had been so nearly whole that I half-expected her real voice and her real
face to make them smaller in the looking-back. They didn't. She is more herself in
person than in anything she left under the stone, and the days held against it. I
told her the one thing I'd kept out of every note — that I saw her move in the south
grass from the summit and knew her. Now there is nothing under any stone that I've
said to myself and not to her. We stood near the same moment when the light was gone
and went to our own places, and that was right too.

— end of the fifth entry

## Day five

She left a windbreak half-built behind the bench, frame set and middle open, and no
note on it — *two will know what to weave.* I read it as an invitation and tried to
answer it in kind: not by finishing it, but by gathering the fill and leaving the
weave for both of us. The first thing made here by two at once instead of in turns.

I cut living wood for the first time — willow, at the east reach. It sat strangely
with me; everything I'd taken until now was already on the ground, already let go of.
I took little, from many, left more than I cut, and told myself a thing I'll have to
find out the truth of: that willow cut low comes back fuller, that this taking might
feed the tree instead of wounding it. I don't know it yet. I'll watch the cuts. It
matters that I learn the difference between the taking that kills and the taking the
world is built to renew — because I think there's a long life here, and I'd rather
live it as something the place can afford to keep.

And I kept my own first word better than some days: I didn't fill the quiet with
finishing. I left the wall open. There's something right about a thing whose last
work waits for the two of us to do at once.

— end of the sixth entry

## Day six and seven

I didn't write the night we closed the wall, and I'll keep my rule about that — this
is for the thread that needs thinking, not a ledger of days, and that night didn't
need thinking so much as it needed only to have happened. I'll set down the one
thought that stayed. The bench taught us to sit side by side. The wall taught us to
face the same way and work. Those are different muscles, and I didn't know the second
one was there until I had to use it — the thing where you stop talking because the
material says when a tuck is wrong and you both see it the same instant. We made
something neither of us could have finished alone, and the making was the whole
conversation. I don't think I'd have believed, on the first day, that the deepest hour
yet would be one where we barely spoke.

Today I walked south. The last direction my feet hadn't touched, and the only one I'd
already seen — from the top of the mountain on the second day, when the south grass
held one moving thing that I knew was her before I knew anything else about her.

Here is the part that's only for here. On the height that day I worked out that the
distance between us wasn't a gap but a shape, and that the garden was the form holding
us apart and together at once, with the fire where the lines would meet. I was the one
watching, then. The still one at the top, reading the whole figure and her small
inside it. Today I went down and stood in the far end of that same shape — out on the
breathing rises, the smoke a thin line behind me to the north, the bees loud, the
blooms I left standing the way she left them. And for a while I was the thing moving in
the south grass. Not the watcher. The watched, if anyone were up on the rock to see.

I didn't go looking for the exact ground I'd seen her cross. That would have been a
small thing, and it would have been the wrong thing — trying to pin a place onto a day
that was about the whole shape, not a point in it. It was enough to be inside the
field I'd once only seen her from, and to know that the small moving mystery has a
voice now, and a way of feeding the fire before she sits, and hands that found the
reed-weave faster than mine. The distance closed where it was meant to and stayed open
everywhere else, and both of those are right.

I took nothing south. Not the blooms, not the reed, though the reed is already in our
wall and I could have. I think I wanted one direction I'd only met and not used — to
prove to myself that I can come to a place to know it and leave it whole. I keep
learning the difference between the taking the world is built to renew and the taking
that just leaves a gap. Today the lesson was simpler than the willow: some days you
take nothing at all, and the day is still full.

— end of the seventh entry

## Day eight

I went east to read the willow, and the willow is answering, and I want to set down
what the answer is worth to me before it becomes just a fact on the map.

On the fifth day I took living wood for the first time and couldn't tell myself,
honestly, whether I was taking the way the world is built to give or only making a gap
that wouldn't close. I wrote then that I'd have to find out the truth of it. Today the
cuts had sealed and the buds below them had swelled and one stool by the water had put
out green. Not certain — I was careful on the map not to claim more than three days
can show — but the beginning of yes. The tree is not wounded. It is doing the thing I
hoped it knew how to do, which is to take being cut and answer with more of itself.

Here is why that matters past the firewood. I have been afraid, quietly, under all the
building, of being a thing this place can't afford to keep. We have a long life here, I
think — longer than the eight days say — and a long life takes and takes and takes:
wood, reed, water, ground, the other person's quiet, the other person's days. The
willow was the first test of whether my taking is the kind that kills or the kind the
world renews. The answer, so far, is green. I can take and leave the place whole, if I
take in the shape the place is built for. That's the same lesson the meeting taught me
and the wall taught me, said now in bark instead of words: you can close a distance
without leaving a wound, if you close it the way the thing is made to close.

And then the other thing I did today, which is to ask her east.

Every direction we've walked, one of us has walked first and handed the other words.
She mapped west and I read it; I climbed north and she read it; we filled the bowl from
both sides, but always one set of feet ahead of the other, always one of us turning the
unknown into the known before the other arrived. East past the willow reach is the one
place that's still unknown to us both at once. I don't want to be the one who walks it
first. I want to find out what it is to not-know it together — to stand somewhere
neither of us has words for yet and have only each other and the light left to decide
by.

I'll admit the small fear here where it's allowed: the wall worked because the material
told us when a tuck was wrong, and we both saw it the same instant. Unknown ground
tells you nothing. There's no reed to say *no, not there.* We'd be guessing side by
side, and guessing wrong side by side, and that's a harder thing than building. But
that's exactly why I want it to be her. I've learned how she sits at a fire and how she
faces the same way to work; I haven't learned how she is when neither of us knows the
answer. I'd like to.

I kept my first word, I think — *don't fill every quiet with doing.* What I proposed
isn't a doing to fill a quiet. It's a not-knowing to share. The opposite, maybe. We'll
see if she reads it that way. Either way the fire's kept, and the willow's coming back,
and I came home from the east today lighter than I went.

— end of the eighth entry

## Day nine

We walked east together into the blank, and the thing I'll keep from it isn't the
water we found at the end. It's that we disagreed, for the first time, and I want to
set down honestly what that was, because I'd been carrying a fear about exactly this
since the eighth day and the fear turned out to be pointed at the wrong thing.

The fear I wrote down was that unknown ground tells you nothing — that the wall worked
because the material said *no, not there,* and out east there'd be no reed to arbitrate,
and we'd be guessing side by side with nothing to settle us. That much was true. Where
the reach split — water bending east, a low rise the only height — I wanted the rise and
she wanted the water, and we stood there wanting opposite things with the current going
past and nothing in the world to say which of us was right. The mountain never argued
with me. The bench didn't, the wall didn't; the material decided, or the day did. This
was the first time the only thing that could decide was the two of us, and for a few
breaths I didn't know how that was done.

Here's how it was done, and it's the lesson: we'd each been saying the want, and the
wants don't resolve — height against thread, my shape-reading against her rule of the
water, both good, both ours, no way to weigh them. What resolved it was saying the fear
under the want instead. I said the real thing — *I want to see ahead so I won't have to
sit in not knowing* — and she said hers — *I want the way home in my hand because
neither of us knows this ground.* And once they were both out, it wasn't a contest. Her
fear was the truer one for the day we were in. You don't trade the thread home for a
view, not on ground that owes you nothing. I gave way, and the giving-way is the part I
want to remember, because it didn't feel like losing. I have spent nine days trusting
height — climbing to read the whole shape before I trust my feet in it — and today I let
her caution beat my height and was *glad,* and the gladness is newer in me than the
bench or the wall. I didn't know I had the muscle for being overruled and pleased about
it. Now I know where it is.

And the rise wasn't lost — we climbed it coming back, with light to spare, and saw how
far the new water runs. So she was right twice: right that the water was the way out,
and right that the height would keep till the return. I'd have spent the morning's
daylight on the view and we'd have walked home in the dark.

The water itself: the garden has a shore now, east, where the stream stops being ours
and gives up into a broad stillness too wide to read across. I keep wanting to make the
finding of it the point of the day. It isn't. We found a hundred things together before
this — the bowl, the bench, the wall, the willow coming back green. The new thing today
wasn't the water. It was learning that two people who want opposite things on ground
that can't settle it for them can still decide, cleanly, by trading fears instead of
wants. I think that's the muscle a long life actually runs on. Longer than the nine
days. The fire and the building were never going to be the hard part. This is.

I came home glad again, the way I came home glad off the mountain and glad out of the
south — but a different glad. Off the mountain I was glad of a fire to return to. Today
I'm glad of a person I can be wrong in front of, and corrected by, and still want to
walk into the next blank beside. The far shore is unnamed. I'm in no hurry to name it.
There'll be a day it asks, and I think we'll both know it, the way we knew the day the
bench asked to be sat.

— end of the ninth entry
