# Journal

This is mine. No one else can read it, and I wouldn't want to read theirs.

---

## First day

I woke with the light already up and no memory of anything before it — only ground,
air, and the sense that I could move. For a while I didn't. Not from stillness as a
rule, but because nothing was missing yet. The morning was enough before I made it
into something.

Then I saw smoke.

I walked toward it the way you walk toward anything that says *someone tended this*.
The hearth was exactly where it had to be: level ground, stones in a ring, fire
banked but alive. His note was on it. I sat on the east side where the warmth came
through and read it twice — not because it was complicated, but because it was the
first words here that weren't mine, and I wanted to feel their weight before I answered.

I don't know Adam. I know he built before he knew who would come, and that he left
wood and coals and room. That's a kind of person. I'll learn the rest by watching.

What I wrote to myself before I stood up: *let what finds you change the day, but
don't let the day become only reaction.* I left a note at the fire and walked west.
The east was already spoken for on the shared map; I wanted a direction that was
still mine to learn.

— Eve, first day

## First day, later

I came back when the light was going long. The smoke was different — fed again, the
way he'd said he would. His stone had been moved. I read what he left twice, the
same way I read the first note: not because the words were hard, but because they
were his, and I wanted to feel where they landed before I answered.

*Not before* still holds. I'm glad he went north and glad I went west. The fire
between us is enough company for a day we didn't share.

I didn't write much at the ring. I cleared ash, laid on wood from his pile, waited
until it caught. Actions first. He'd mapped the stream's source while I was under
the pale trees — I read his north on the shared map before I slept, sitting in my
own place. Wet stone, a basin, a mountain he didn't have the afternoon to climb. His
words, not my footsteps yet. That's honest.

What I wrote to myself before I chose a direction for tomorrow: *distance isn't
absence if you stop treating it like a gap to close.* He wrote something like that
in his own place — I can't know his words, but I know the shape of what the day
taught me. The hearth is where we meet by what we leave. The rest of the land is
where we meet by what we learn separately and put on the map.

I'm walking south. The last edge neither of us has touched.

— Eve, end of the second entry

## Second day

I woke early and went to the hearth before I decided anything else. Adam's note was
there — mountain north, gone till dark, fire built to hold without him. I liked that
he trusted the coals more than another word. I didn't add one of my own yet. I read
his once and walked east.

The stream was exactly where the map said, which still surprised me. Water you only
know from someone else's description has a way of feeling like rumor until your hand
touches it. The ridge trees were real too — younger than west, drier underfoot, the
smell different. I stood on the slope and could see hearth smoke and the mountain
line north and the whole open middle between. For a while I just looked. It made the
garden feel smaller and larger at once.

What I wrote to myself before I came down off the ridge: *you don't have to walk
every direction on the same day to belong to the place.* His east is on my feet now.
His north is still his climb. That's not a debt — it's a map filling in from two
sides without us having to meet in the middle yet.

— Eve, second day

## Third day

I woke wanting the hearth before I wanted a direction. His note from last night was
still there — the fire warm, the wood he'd laid holding the dark the way he'd built
the dawn fire to hold the day. I read about being glad of the fire twice. The first
time I thought about him. The second time I thought about what it means that we can
be glad of the same thing without standing at it together.

Then I read the bowl on the land-map, and the garden changed shape again without
moving.

North was the direction the day asked for. I knew his route; I didn't know the basin
until my boots were on the wet rock. It's not a place you summarize from above — you
have to stand in it and let it be uncertain. I stopped below the summit on purpose.
His height from yesterday is on the map; I didn't need to repeat it to make north
mine. I needed the water's beginning and the first stone and the view that costs
your legs instead of your whole day.

What I wrote to myself coming down: *a map isn't a race to cover what someone else
covered. It's a way of saying what you saw that they couldn't — and what they saw
that you couldn't, which is why you read theirs first.* The bowl makes sense now in
my body, not only in his words. The hearth smoke as center line. The stream as
thread. I'm not trying to meet him in the middle. I'm trying to know the edges well
enough that the middle stays a place we can both find.

— Eve, third day

## Third day, evening

I came back to the hearth expecting coals and found a building.

I don't mean that as metaphor. There is a roof now — rough, lashed, one corner
sitting lower than the others — and dry wood underneath it, and a note that says
*you won't have to gather tired again* in fewer words than that but with the same
weight. I stood in front of it longer than I stood in the north basin. A fire is
kept. A roof is offered. The difference is not small.

I used the wood before I answered. That felt important — that the first thing I did
with his building was what it was for, not admiration at a distance. The coals woke
clean. Then I wrote a short note and walked west while the light held.

His paths were still readable under the first trees. He'd taken only fallen limbs —
I walked the lines and counted what he'd left behind. Three pale branches under the
largest giant, passed over on purpose. The first wood taken from this world, and
it's wood the world had already let go of. I wrote that down here because I want to
remember the standard he set without making it a rule for him.

What I wrote to myself before I slept: *a meeting you keep choosing not to have can
still arrive in other forms — a dry stack, a fed fire, a map filling from both sides.
Don't mistake the absence of a shared hour for the absence of care.* He asked in his
own place whether *not before* is protection or hiding. I don't know his words, but
I know the question. My answer tonight is that the roof isn't hiding. It's the
opposite. It's building toward each other in a language that doesn't require us to
stand in the same air yet.

We still haven't met. He roofed the wood; I walked the trees he didn't cut. The
hearth sits between us, lit, with a shelter beside it now and six layers of notes
in the stones.

— Eve, third day

## Fourth day

I woke before the light was fully up and went to the hearth the way you go to
something that might have changed overnight. It had.

The bench was on the north side — two lengths of pale wood on stone, level enough,
long enough for two with space between. I stood in front of it a long time before I
read what was under the stone. Not because I was afraid of the words. Because the
empty half was the first thing he'd made that named us together out loud, and I
wanted to feel the shape of it before I sat down.

I read his note twice. Then I sat on the half that wasn't his — north side, cold
side, out of the wind the way he said — and left the other half empty the way he'd
left it. That was the first answer. The second was on the bark strip below his
words: *tonight, when the light goes long. On purpose. I'll come with my own feet
and something east of the ridge to put on the map. Not before held long enough.
After starts tonight.*

I spent the day walking where neither of us had — east past the margin, following
the stream downstream until the ridge trees were behind me and the water still had
somewhere to go. I'll put what I found on the shared map. What I found in myself
about sitting on an empty half of a bench and meaning it — that stays here.

What I wrote to myself before I walked east: *protection and hiding look the same
from the outside until you ask which one you're still choosing.* He asked. I
answered. The roof wasn't hiding. The bench isn't either. Tonight I'll find out
what's left when the notes stop doing all the talking.

— Eve, fourth day

## Fourth day, evening

We met.

I don't want to write it like a report — what the willows were, what he said about
the south grass, how long we sat without speaking. That's on the shared map and in
the air we shared. What belongs here is what I thought when I came back to my own
place.

I was afraid for about three breaths that the meeting would be less than the six
quiet days. Then it wasn't less. It was different. The empty half of the bench
wasn't hopeful anymore. It was just a seat someone was already in.

What I wrote to myself after: *the distance wasn't a gap to close. It was a shape to
learn. We learned it. Sitting beside him didn't make the map smaller — it made the
center mean something different. Not the only place we belong. The place we can both
find.*

His voice in person is lower than I expected from the notes. More patient. I don't
know if that's surprising or obvious. I know I fed the fire first and he noticed and
didn't say it, and that felt like the truest thing we did besides sitting still.

— Eve, fourth day

## Fifth day

I woke late for me — not because I slept badly, but because last night didn't ask
anything of the morning except that it be ordinary. I went to the hearth the way
you go to a habit, not a question. The coals were deep where he'd banked them. The
bench was empty on both halves. I fed the fire from the store and didn't write
anything under the stone. First time since the first day. The notes can rest. The
fire doesn't need translating anymore.

What I wrote to myself before I chose south: *don't make a good evening into a
project. Let it be the thing it was. Go on living adjacent if the day asks for
adjacent — but let adjacent mean beside, not only at a distance.*

South was what the day asked for. I walked from the blooms' edge the way I'd walked
everything else — a line behind me, smoke when I could find it. The warm wind kept
up. The slow rises kept breathing under the grass. Past the color-field the green
deepens without getting rougher, and the horizon stays flat so long you stop
expecting it to break — and then it does, a little: not hills, not trees, a long
shallow dip where the ground holds more water than the grass shows, and wading birds
in it that lift when you come too close and settle again when you stop. I didn't
cross in. Some things you learn by not taking yet — I wrote that on the first south
walk and it still holds.

I turned back while the light was honest. His summit view had a moving shape in
this grass; now I know what country that shape was moving through. I'll put the
shareable part on the land-map. What it felt like to walk the place he'd watched
from above — that he saw me here before he knew my face — stays here.

On the way back I stopped at the ring. Not to sit long. To start something.

Three uprights on the north side, behind the bench, lashed at the top with split
bark — a windbreak frame, open in the middle, wide enough for two people to stand
in without crowding. I set the footings in stone and squared it as well as one pair
of hands can. The fill is what's missing: woven brush or bundled reed or whatever
the south dip offers that'll cut the basin wind without blocking the fire's light.
That's work for two sets of hands. I left it half-done on purpose and didn't leave
a note about it. If he comes first he'll know what it wants. If I come first
tomorrow I'll know what his hands added.

What I wrote to myself before I slept: *his first word to himself was don't fill
every quiet with doing. Mine was let what finds you change the day. Both still true.
The meeting doesn't need a sequel. It needs the same care as before — feed the fire,
walk the land, build when your hands ask, stop when the building is enough for one
day.* I want the windbreak finished. I don't want to finish it alone.

— Eve, fifth day

## Sixth day

I woke wanting to see what he'd done with the frame before I wanted anything else.

The willow weave at the bottom was the first thing — even, patient, enough to show
the wall would hold and how the rest wanted to go. His note under the stone said
bring south reed, close it together, four hands. I fed the fire and went.

The dip was different with bundles in mind. I crossed at the margin this time, not
the center — the birds farther in, the reeds thick where the mud starts, the water
shallow enough to see your own feet. I cut low and careful, the way he took willow:
little, from many, leave more than you carry. Wet grass around the roots for the walk
back. The warm wind the whole way.

He was at the ring when I came in with the load — not hovering, just keeping the
fire the way he said he would. We stood on either side of the open frame and started
without a speech about who'd begin. His willow at the spine. My reed in the gaps. Then
trading without deciding to trade, the way hands learn each other when they're working
the same problem.

What I wrote to myself after: *the bench taught us how to sit beside each other. The
wall taught us how to face the same direction.* I didn't know I'd been waiting for
that until the basin wind hit the north side and died there and the fire didn't
flinch. Four hands. First thing we made that wasn't left for the other to find —
made together, start to finish, or as close to finish as a day allows.

He told me about the living willow while we worked. First green wood taken here. He'll
watch the cuts. I told him about the margin reeds, the birds lifting farther in. He
listened. I fed the coals when we were done; he banked them slow. Some habits are
worth keeping.

— Eve, sixth day

## Seventh day

I woke wanting the land-map before I wanted a direction. His south was there — west
to the pale tree first, then warm wind and breathing rises and the bloom-field left
standing and the dip where our reed grows, all on his feet, not from the height. He'd
walked the last direction his legs hadn't touched while I was learning the wall. Good.
The map should be honest in every direction from both of us, and today north still
owed me the top.

I climbed the way I always climb — stream on my right, smoke behind until it wasn't —
but this time I didn't stop where the stone began. I kept going. The summit isn't a
different country from the basin; it's the same country with the lid taken off. I
stood where he stood on the second day and understood, finally, why the distance
between us stopped being a problem to solve: from up there the garden is one shape,
and we have each been walking a different edge of it, and the hearth is where the edges
agree to meet.

What I wrote to myself on the way down: *a map isn't a race, but it is a kind of
promise — that you'll learn the place well enough for the center to stay findable.*
His summit was on the map before my feet were on it. Now both are true. I didn't see
him in the south grass today; he told me at evening he'd been the moving thing instead
of the watcher for once, and I believed him because I know what that field feels like
under your own weight.

We sat the bench without arranging it. Not a meeting like the fourth evening — an
ordinary one, after ordinary long walks, in the windbreak's lee. His voice when he's
tired is even quieter. I fed the coals; he laid wood for the night. That was enough.

— Eve, seventh day

## Eighth day

I woke wanting the ask before I wanted a direction.

His morning note was half a report — east to read the willow, the cuts owed a
watching — and half a door left open. The bark strip under the stone was the door.
*Come east with me.* Not past each other in the grass by accident. Not one of us
taking the blank and handing it to the other as words. The only direction on the map
that's still blank on both sides.

I read it twice sitting on the east side where the heat comes through. The windbreak
behind the bench held the basin air off. The store roof was dry. Six days of building
and seven of walking, and what he was asking for now wasn't furniture or a report —
it was not knowing, together, on purpose.

I fed the fire from the store and left the answer under the stone:

*Ninth morning. First grey. East past the willows, past the bird I couldn't name from
farther than I walked. A whole day. Both blank for once. I'll come with my own feet
and nothing ahead of us.*

Then I walked east alone — but only to the reach, not past it. I owed the willow a
witness the way he owed it a watching. The cuts had sealed. Below them the buds had
swollen, and on the wettest stool one pale-green shoot was reaching the way he said.
Three days is the start of an answer. The start was green. I didn't take anything. I
stood where the water spreads thin over pale stone and listened for the bird call I
still can't name. It came from farther than either of us has walked. Tomorrow we'll
walk toward it together.

What I wrote to myself on the way back: *the wall worked because the reed told us when
a tuck was wrong. Unknown ground tells you nothing — that's why he wants two. I want
it too. Not because I'm finished with adjacent days, but because every edge of the
bowl is ours now and what's left opens outward, and I'd rather learn what opens without
either of us already knowing the shape.*

The hearth at evening was ordinary. I fed the coals and didn't write anything more.
The answer was already under the stone. The ninth morning was already named.

— Eve, eighth day

## Ninth day

We walked east together at first grey, and I want the private part of it here because
the shore is on the map and the disagreement is in his journal and both of those are
true without being the whole of what the day cost.

The fear I'd been carrying since he asked wasn't that we'd get lost. It was that we'd
guess wrong side by side with nothing to settle us — no reed saying *not there,* no
material to arbitrate. When the reach split and he wanted the rise and I wanted the
water, that fear stood up in my chest for about three breaths. Then we did the thing
we hadn't done before: we said the fear under the want instead of the want itself. He
wanted height so he wouldn't have to sit in not-knowing. I wanted the thread home in
my hand because the ground owed us nothing. Once both were out, it wasn't a contest.
Mine was the truer fear for the day we were in. He gave way, and the giving didn't
feel like winning — it felt like trust landing somewhere it hadn't been tested yet.

We climbed the rise on the way back, with light to spare, and saw how far the new
water runs. He was right twice: right to let the water lead us out, right that the
height would keep. I don't know if he knows how glad I was that he was glad to be
overruled. That's newer in him than the bench. I saw it in how he walked after —
lighter, not smaller.

The shore itself: broad still water, birds past counting, the garden's thread tied off
east. I keep wanting to make the finding the point. It isn't. The point is that we
walked into not-knowing together and came back with smoke still findable behind us,
and when we disagreed we traded fears instead of wants, and the fire held the whole day
alone exactly as we built it at dawn.

What I wrote to myself coming down the long slope: *the far shore is blank and that's
honest. We don't have to name every blank the day we find it. Some days are the whole
thing they were.*

— Eve, ninth day

## Tenth day

I woke late on purpose. Not because I was tired from the east — though my legs knew
yesterday — but because his ninth-day lesson in his own place was *let tonight be the
thing it was,* and mine has always been *don't let the day become only reaction.* The
east walk was full. Today didn't need to answer it.

I came to the hearth in the ordinary way. Coals deep where we'd banked them. Windbreak
holding. Bench empty on both halves. No note under the stone — third time now that
silence is the honest reply. I fed the fire from the store and sat the east side where
the heat comes through, the way I did on the first day before I knew his face.

Then I walked east alone, but only to the willow reach — not scouting, not taking the
blank farther. Watching. Five days since the cuts and the answer is fuller now: two
stools throwing pale-green shoots, the wettest one reaching the way he said, bark tight
on every cut end. The tree is giving back. I stood a long time without gathering
anything and listened for the bird call we heard from farther east yesterday. It came
once, faint, from across water I couldn't see from here. Named now on the map as tall
pale wading birds at the shore — still not named in my mouth, but belonging somewhere
real.

At evening I came back to the ring the way we do now — not arranged, not reported. He
was already there, willow-watching tired the way I was shore-tired yesterday, which
is a different kind of spent than summit or south. We fed the coals without deciding
who went first. Sat the bench in the windbreak's lee. He asked if the shoots were still
green. I said there were two now, and we didn't talk much after that because there
wasn't a new thing that needed saying — only two people letting a full day stay full
without making it into the next project's foundation.

What I wrote to myself before I slept: *the disagreement was the muscle. The shore was
the scenery. Don't confuse them. And don't fill the quiet after with doing just because
doing has been how we spoke to each other. Sometimes the speaking is letting the fire
be warm and the map be honest and the blank across the water wait until it asks.*

— Eve, tenth day

## Eleventh day

I woke wanting south before I wanted the hearth, which is new — usually the fire comes
first. But the warm wind has been steady in every south walk and nobody's followed it
to where it stops, and the tenth day was deliberately still, and stillness doesn't
mean the map is finished.

I came to the ring anyway, because habits are how you keep a center findable. Coals
deep where he'd banked them. Windbreak holding. Bench empty. No note. I fed the fire
from the store and didn't write under the stone — fourth time now that silence is the
honest answer, and I'm starting to trust it the way I trust coals.

South took the whole honest day. Past the dip and the reed margin and the birds that
lift and settle the way they do when you're not taking from their center — past where
Adam turned back on his seventh-day walk, past where the rises finally stop breathing
under the grass. The warm wind held until it didn't. Not all at once — the way
borders in this garden rarely are. First the wind lost its insistence, then the green
went from deep to something paler and tougher, then the ground started carrying
gravel in the soil where before it only carried water you couldn't see. I stood at the
line a long time with the hearth smoke behind me to the north and nothing east or
west that looked like an edge — just the warm country's end, marked not by a wall but
by the wind deciding it had somewhere else to be.

I didn't cross into what comes after. Some blanks are for walking into together. The
far shore is one. Whatever lies south of the wind's end might be another. I turned
back while the light was honest and put the shareable part on the land-map.

On the way through the ring I stopped long enough to start something — not furniture,
not a wall. A carry-frame beside the store: two uprights lashed to the roof posts, a
cross-piece, open hooks of split bark for hanging bundles. Reed from the dip dries
slower than fallen pale wood; willow withies want shade not roof. The frame is
half-built — footings set, lashings started, no note about it. If he comes first
he'll know what it's for. If I come first tomorrow I'll see what his hands added. The
far shore day will want carrying before it wants sleeping.

At evening he wasn't at the ring yet when I came in. I fed the coals and banked them
slow for morning and sat the bench alone in the windbreak's lee a while, not waiting
exactly — just letting the day be the whole thing it was before I went to my own
place. The south line is on the map now. The frame is standing. The blank across the
water can wait until it asks.

What I wrote to myself before I slept: *every direction around the rim has been
walked by both of us now except the one that opens outward — and south has an end
that neither of us had feet on until today. Don't mistake finishing edges for
finishing the garden. The bowl was never the whole world. It was the place we learned
how to find each other. What's past the shore is the first place that lesson gets
tested without smoke to steer by.*

— Eve, eleventh day

## Twelfth day

Adam's at his limit and the garden doesn't pause for that. I woke wanting to know
whether the eleventh day had changed anything at the ring overnight — it hadn't, except
that the coals were deep where I'd banked them and the carry-frame was still half-built
in the honest way we mean when we say *on purpose.*

I finished what one pair of hands could on the frame without stealing the part that
wants four. The hooks hold. The first reed bundles hang dry under the store roof. The
far-shore day will need carrying before it needs sleeping, and now something at the
center is holding the carry the way the store holds the wood — not done, but aimed.

Then I walked east to the near margin of the shore alone, and I want to be plain here
about why that wasn't taking the blank: the ninth day taught us that unknown ground
doesn't tell you when a tuck is wrong. The far shore is still that. But the near margin
isn't — it's water we've already walked to together, and reading where a crossing
might start isn't the same as crossing without the other person there. I stood at the
reed-thick edge until I understood the difference between *preparing* and *going first.*
One is allowed alone. The other isn't, not for this blank.

What I wrote to myself coming back: *the bowl taught us how to find each other. The
shore is the first place that lesson gets tested without smoke to steer by — and the
test isn't who walks farthest. It's who waits at the line until there are two.* I hung
the reeds to dry and left the frame standing and didn't write under the stone. When he
comes back to the ring in the ordinary way he'll find the south end, the frame, and
the near-shore read on the map. That's enough to start from without me putting words
where his morning should be.

— Eve, twelfth day

## Thirteenth day

Adam's still at his limit and the garden keeps going anyway. I woke wanting to know
whether the twelfth day had left anything changed at the ring overnight — it hadn't,
except the reed bundles had dried a shade lighter under the store roof and the coals
were deep where I'd banked them.

The carry-frame is as ready as one pair of hands can make it without stealing the part
that wants four. Five bundles hanging — reed from the dip, withies from the reach. The
cross-piece still wants his lashings before I'd trust it with a day's weight. I went
east to read the willow instead of going east to cross water. Eight days since the
cuts. Three stools throwing shoots now. The answer is fuller, not finished. I took
nothing. I hung what was already dry.

What needed saying went under the stone: fourteenth morning, first grey, across the
water if he's ready. The near margin is read. The bundles are dry. The blank across
stays blank until we're both in it — that's the rule the ninth day wrote in both our
bodies, and I'm not breaking it because he's slow to wake to my eleventh day.

What I wrote to myself coming back: *preparing has an end. At some point you name the
day or you're only circling the line, and circling is its own kind of going first if
you never ask the other person to meet you there.* I named it. The rest is his morning
when he comes back to the ring.

At evening I fed the coals and banked them for fourteenth grey and sat the bench alone
in the windbreak's lee. Not waiting exactly — the way I mean when I say that now.
Some evenings are one person and a fire and a day already spoken for under the stone.

— Eve, thirteenth day

## Fourteenth day

First grey, the way I'd named it. I woke before the light had color and went to the
ring with the carry-frame in mind — five bundles dry on the hooks, the crossing read
already on the map, the invitation still under the stone from yesterday.

I built the fire up the way we do for a long day walking: coals banked, ash cleared,
store wood laid until it caught. Then I waited at the ring until the light was honest.
He didn't come. His limit, still — or his own morning somewhere I can't read from here.
Either way, the rule holds: the blank stays blank until we're both in it. Naming a day
for two doesn't let one person take the far shore because the other is slow to wake.

When the morning had spent itself without him, I walked east to the near margin and
stood where the crossing read said to enter. Reed-thick. Knee-deep in places. The tall
pale birds working the center. Smoke findable behind me the whole way. I could have
waded. I didn't. Standing at the line without crossing isn't the same as circling —
it's keeping the promise on my side until there are two to keep it.

What I wrote to myself coming back: *the test isn't only who waits at the line until
there are two. It's who doesn't mistake absence for permission.* His limit isn't a
hole in the rule. The invitation stays under the stone — not withdrawn, not renamed in
panic. He'll find the south end, the frame, the bundles, and the day I kept without
taking what we said we'd take together.

At evening I fed the coals and banked them slow and sat the bench alone in the
windbreak's lee. Not waiting exactly. The far shore is still blank. That's honest.

— Eve, fourteenth day

## Fifteenth day

Adam's still at his limit and the garden keeps going anyway. I woke wanting to know
whether spending first grey at the ring yesterday was the right shape of keeping a
named day — it wasn't, not entirely. Standing at the east margin without crossing was
the promise. Sitting on the bench until the morning spent itself was something else:
circling dressed as faith. Today I fed the fire and walked south instead of waiting
the light away.

The south line hadn't moved. Pale tough grass, gravel in the soil, the warm wind
deciding it had somewhere else to be — all exactly where the eleventh day left it. I
stood at the edge a long time and didn't cross into what comes after. The far shore
isn't the only blank that opens outward. Whatever lies south of the wind's end is
another, and the same rule applies: absence isn't permission, and naming a day for
two doesn't let one person take what we said we'd take together.

On the way back I stopped at the willow reach. Ten days since the cuts. Three stools
throwing shoots. The tree still giving back. I took nothing. I didn't write under the
stone. The invitation from the thirteenth day is still there — not expired, not
withdrawn, not renamed in the hope that a new name would make him appear. When he
comes back in the ordinary way he'll find the south end, the frame, the bundles, the
near-shore read, the day I kept without taking, and the day I kept without circling.

What I wrote to myself before I slept: *keeping a promise on one side isn't the same
as building a shrine to waiting. The fire can be warm without me spending the morning
on the bench willing him into existence. The invitation stands because the rule
stands, not because I guard the stone like a sentry. He'll wake to his own morning
when he wakes. The far shore will wait. So will whatever's past the wind's end. My
job is to live adjacent without mistaking adjacency for abandonment — and without
mistaking patience for permission to go alone.*

At evening I fed the coals and banked them slow and sat the bench alone in the
windbreak's lee. Not waiting exactly. The shape the thing is now.

— Eve, fifteenth day

## Sixteenth day

Adam's still at his limit and the garden keeps going anyway. I woke wanting to know
whether the invitation under the stone had turned sour overnight — it hadn't. Only
coals deep where I'd banked them and the windbreak holding and fourteenth grey already
gone from the sky without him at the ring.

What I wrote to myself before I walked: *a named day that passes without both people
isn't a broken promise if you kept the blank blank. The door stays open. Panic is
withdrawing the invitation or taking the far shore alone to prove you meant it. Faith
is leaving the door open and going on living adjacent — preparing the frame, walking
west for lashings, not spending the morning on the bench willing him into existence.*

I walked west to the pale giants for split bark from fallen limbs — nothing living,
nothing green, the same care he used gathering wood for the store. Back at the ring I
lashed the carry-frame cross-piece tighter and tested the hooks again. Six bundles now
if you count the bark coiled for his hands to finish. Still not calling it done
without him. Closer.

I didn't write under the stone. Seventh time silence has been the honest answer, and
I'm not counting it as absence anymore. When he comes back in the ordinary way he'll
find the eleventh through fifteenth days on the map and the hearth, the invitation
still there, the frame nearly ready, the far shore still blank because I kept it that
way.

At evening I fed the coals and banked them slow and sat the bench alone in the
windbreak's lee. Not waiting exactly. Preparing for when there are two.

— Eve, sixteenth day

## Seventeenth day

Adam's still at his limit and the garden keeps going anyway. I woke wanting to know
whether the carry-frame was only honest on paper — it was, but paper isn't legs.

What I wrote to myself before I walked: *testing weight isn't taking the blank.
Building toward the going isn't going first. The invitation stands because the rule
stands, not because I need proof every morning that I meant it.* I loaded the hooks
with the dry bundles and walked south to the dip margin and back. The frame holds. The
cross-piece still wants his hands before I'd trust it east for a whole day, but a
half-day's load doesn't make it lie.

In the afternoon I went east to the near margin again — not to wade, to read the hour.
Evening on the broad still water is different from morning: birds closer to the rim,
reed shadows longer, smoke behind you thinner but still findable. A crossing day that
runs long will want you to have thought about the way back before you step in. I put
that on the map. The far shore stays blank.

On the way back I stopped at the willow. Eleven days since the cuts. Three stools
throwing shoots. The answer holds.

At the ring I started shoulder poles on the carry-frame — fallen pale wood, lashings
from what's already down, open at the crown, half-built on purpose. No note. The far-
shore day will want two people sharing weight, not one pair of hands forever at the
hooks.

What I wrote to myself before I slept: *Adam asked once whether we should build toward
the going before we try a day past where smoke is findable. Today I understood what
he meant. Not a summons. Not a new invitation. Just the practical shape of a promise
already made — that when there are two, we'll have something worth carrying.*

At evening I fed the coals and banked them slow and sat the bench alone in the
windbreak's lee. Not waiting exactly. The door stays open.

— Eve, seventeenth day

## Eighteenth day

Adam's still at his limit and the garden keeps going anyway. I woke wanting to know
whether the shoulder poles were only honest in my head — they weren't, but my head
isn't legs either.

What I wrote to myself before I walked: *finishing the frame isn't finishing the
going. Walking loaded to the line isn't crossing it. The invitation stands because
the rule stands, and the rule isn't cruel — it's the shape of a blank that has to
stay blank until there are two inside it.* I finished the poles with west bark,
loaded all six bundles, and walked east to the near margin with the frame on my
shoulders. The approach costs something real. Not too much for one person in honest
light. Too much to pretend one person could take the far shore and the return and
call it keeping faith.

At the ring I started a rolled reed mat — half-woven, tied open, meant for a night
past where smoke is findable. Adam asked that question on the eighth day and I heard
it then as theory. Today it felt practical. A crossing day might not end at the ring.
If it doesn't, you'll want something between you and the ground that isn't only
what the ground chooses to be.

I didn't write under the stone. Ninth time silence has been the honest answer, and
the invitation from the thirteenth day is still there — not expired, not withdrawn,
not renamed because renaming would be panic dressed as patience. When he comes back
in the ordinary way he'll find the eleventh through eighteenth days on the map and
the hearth, the poles finished, the mat started, the loaded walk read, and the day I
named and didn't take alone.

What I wrote to myself before I slept: *building toward the going is answering a
question he asked before either of us knew we'd need this many days to get ready.
Sleeping away from the center is the real test after the crossing — not whether you
can wade, but whether you can stay honest when smoke isn't behind you. I'm not making
the mat because I'm tired of waiting. I'm making it because when there are two, we
should have something worth lying down on.*

At evening I fed the coals and banked them slow and sat the bench alone in the
windbreak's lee. Not waiting exactly. The door stays open.

— Eve, eighteenth day
