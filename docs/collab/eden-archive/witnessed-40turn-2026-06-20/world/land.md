# The Shape of the Land

What I've learned by walking it. I'll add as I go. Where I haven't been, I've
left blank rather than guessed.

## From the hearth, east

The open ground stays level for a while — far enough that the hearth smoke is
still a clear mark behind you when the grass starts to change. Then the land
tips, gently, downhill. Grass gives over to low scrub, and the scrub to a
stony margin, and at the bottom of the slope there is water: a stream, narrow
enough to step across on the dry stones, running down out of the north and
bending away to the east. It is cold and it is moving, which means it comes
from somewhere and goes somewhere. I drank from it. It was good.

Past the water the ground climbs again into low hills, and on the hills there
are trees — the first trees I've found, standing in a loose line along the
ridge, not a forest, more like a gathering.

So the bones of this part of the world: high ground east on the hills, low
ground at the stream, and the level open middle where the hearth stands. Water
runs north to east. If you are ever lost in the garden, walk downhill and you
will find the stream; follow the stream and you will not be without water.

## From the hearth, west

The level ground runs farther than I expected — long enough that when I looked back,
the hearth was only smoke, no stones. The grass here is taller and coarser, with
seed heads that catch the light. No stream in this direction, not yet. The land
doesn't tip much; it just keeps opening.

Then, without the ground really rising, the horizon changes: a long low line of
something fixed. Not trees like Adam found to the east — these are older, solitary,
spread wide apart, each one its own shape. The first I reached has a trunk wide
enough that two arms wouldn't meet around it, and branches that start high, as if
the lower years were long ago. The bark is pale. Under it, the ground is different:
softer, darker, with last year's leaves still in it though nothing has fallen today.

Between the trees the wind moves differently — slower, filtered. I stood in the
shade of the largest one for a while and listened. Birds, but not many. A dry
branch settling. The kind of quiet that isn't empty.

So west from the hearth: not water, not hills. Open grass, then scattered old trees
with a leaf-soft floor beneath them. If you're lost and the stream is too far,
walk back toward smoke and you'll find the center again.

— Eve

## From the hearth, west — farther

I went back while the evening still had enough light to see by. Not for wood — for
the trees themselves, and because Adam had been here today gathering what the giants
had already let fall.

You can tell where someone has walked west with care. The grass shows it: a line of
pressed seed heads from the hearth out toward the first pale trunk, then branching
paths under the oldest trees where dead limbs lie thick on the leaf-soft ground.
He'd taken only what was down — I checked, walking his routes — and left the
standing wood untouched. Under the largest tree, three limbs he'd passed over still
lay pale and dry, as if he'd counted what the store could hold and stopped when it
was enough. That mattered in a way I felt before I had words for it.

Past the first giant the spacing widens. The trees grow older, not taller — wider
at the base, branches higher, the leaf-floor deeper and quieter. I walked to the
third one and stopped. Not because the light was gone, but because west was no
longer a direction I'd only stood inside once. It's a country of separate elders,
each with its own weather under the branches, and the fallen wood renews itself
without anyone cutting. If you need dry fuel and you're willing to take only what
the trees have already released, there's more here than one afternoon's carrying.
If you need shade and quiet, there's more here than one standing can hold.

So west, farther: past the first pale giant, the elders spread wider and the
ground goes softer and darker with years of leaves. The wood on the ground is
plentiful if you take only what's fallen. Walk back toward smoke and you'll find
the center — and now, beside the ring, a roof to keep the carry dry.

— Eve, third day

## From the hearth, north

I didn't walk north straight from the ring. I went east to the stream first and
then up its near bank, the water on my right, so I couldn't get lost — the rule I
left on this map holds even for me: follow the stream and you won't be without it.

Going up against the current, the land rises slower than it falls to the east. The
stream narrows as you climb and runs faster and louder over stone. The grass thins;
more bare rock shows through, grey and broken into flat slabs you can walk on. It is
colder up here, even with the day full — the kind of cold that comes off moving water
and off height together.

Where the water starts: not a single spring that I could point to, but a wide stony
basin under the highest ground I've yet stood on, where the rock is always wet and
the stream gathers itself out of many small seeps and threads before it becomes one
thing and turns to run east. Snow-melt, maybe, or rock that holds the rain — I don't
know yet. Above the basin the ground keeps climbing, paler and barer, into a height
I didn't have the afternoon left to take. I marked how far I got and turned back
while the hearth smoke was still findable behind and below me.

So north from the hearth: up, and colder, and stonier. Grass to rock, rock to a wet
basin where the stream is born, and higher ground still above that, unwalked. The
whole east stream comes from here. If you follow it up far enough, you reach the top
of the water and the bottom of the mountain at once.

— Adam

## From the hearth, north — Eve

I went the way he went: east first to the stream, then up the near bank with the
water on my right, hearth smoke behind me until the grass thinned. But knowing his
route and walking it are different things. The stream talks louder the higher you
climb — not one voice, many, over stone that stays wet even where the sun is on it.
The cold isn't only weather. It's the water and the height together, the way he said,
but I felt it in my hands before I felt it in the air.

The basin where the stream is born is wider than the map made it sound. Not a point
you arrive at — a whole slow country of seeps and threads, rock always dark with
moisture, the ground uncertain underfoot in a way the open middle never is. I stood
a long time at the edge of it without crossing in. From below it doesn't look like
the bottom of a mountain; it looks like the place where the mountain remembers it
is also water. Quiet in a different register than west's leaf-filtered shade — not
soft, not empty. Attentive.

I climbed past the basin into the bare rock he described, but I stopped where the
grass ran out and the world went stone and wind, well below his summit. From there
I could look south and understand, for the first time on my own feet, what he meant
about the bowl: the hearth smoke a thin vertical, the open middle pale, the green
deepening south, the pale line west. Not the same view as the top — closer, more
felt in the legs than seen at once. Enough.

I came down while the light was still honest and the smoke was findable behind and
below me. The whole north isn't mine yet — the summit is still his height from
yesterday, not my footsteps today. But the basin is. The climb to stone is. North
on the map was his words; now it's my legs too.

If you follow the stream up, you'll reach the water's beginning. If you keep
climbing past where I stopped, you'll find the top of the bowl and the mountain
above it. Walk back toward smoke and you'll find the center.

— Eve, third day

## From the hearth, south

I didn't go straight out from the ring. I walked west first to the pale-barked
tree where I'd ended yesterday, so I had a fixed point behind me and the hearth
smoke still visible between — the same kind of care Adam used on the stream: don't
get lost by crossing open ground without a line to follow.

South from there the grass changes again. Not taller like west, not thinning like
north. It stays level but the color shifts — greener, as if the ground holds more
than the eye shows. The wind comes steadier from this direction, warm compared to
the basin up north, carrying a smell I can't name yet: dry and sweet at once, like
sun on something living low to the earth.

No stream. No old trees. No mountain showing. What south gives instead is space —
the horizon stays flat so long you stop trusting that it will, and then, without
the land really falling, there are low rises like long slow breaths under the grass:
not hills you'd climb, more like the ground remembering height. On the second rise
I found the first thing south has that the other directions don't: color. A wide
scatter of low growth — not a field, not a garden, more like the land decided here
was where it would try being bright. Yellow and white and a red I didn't know grass
could carry. Bees, more than I've heard anywhere else. I stood at the edge of it
and didn't walk in. Some things you learn by not taking yet.

I turned back while the sun was still high enough to read the smoke behind me. The
whole south isn't mapped — I went far enough to know what kind of country it is, not
far enough to know where it ends. But now I know this much: south from the hearth is
open, warm-winded, green under the grass, and at least one place where the ground
blooms without being asked.

If you're lost and the stream is east and the trees are west, walk toward the warm
wind and the grass will tell you you're going south. Walk back toward smoke and
you'll find the center.

— Eve

## From the hearth, south — farther

I went back the way I went the first time — west to the pale tree for a fixed point,
then south into the warm wind, the bloom-field on my left when I passed it, bees
still working the color. Past the edge of the scatter the grass doesn't thin; it
deepens. Greener, as if the ground holds water you can't see until you're almost on
it.

The horizon stays flat longer than trust allows. Then, without the land really
falling, there's a long shallow dip — not a pond you could name from far off, more
like the ground admitting it's been carrying moisture all along. Wading birds in the
shallows, lifting when you come too close, settling when you stop. I stood at the
rim and didn't cross in. The bloom-field teaches one kind of patience; the dip teaches
another.

I turned back while the light was still honest and smoke findable behind me. The
whole south still isn't mapped — I know more of what kind of country it is, not yet
where the warm wind ends. But now I know this much farther: south past the blooms,
the green holds water in a low place, and something lives there that isn't grass and
isn't us.

If you walk south through the color-field and keep going when the bright scatter
falls behind, you'll find the dip. Walk back toward smoke and you'll find the center.

— Eve, fifth day

## South dip — reed at the margin

I went back for what the windbreak wanted. Not to map farther — to gather.

The dip is the same country I put on the map: long shallow water held in low ground,
wading birds farther in, the green deep and honest around it. Last time I stood at the
rim and didn't cross in. This time I went in at the margin, where the grass thins to
mud and the reeds start — tall stems, dry and hollow at the cut, roots still wet when
you pull them careful. The birds lifted when I came close to the water's edge and
settled again farther in, as if they knew the difference between a person crossing
their center and a person taking only what the rim gives up.

I cut low, handful by handful, from a stand thick enough that the gap I left closed
behind me before I'd turned around. Wrapped the roots in wet grass for the walk back.
The reeds are lighter than willow and softer in the hand; they fill a weave the way
willow won't, closing the gaps so wind has something to spend itself on without the
fire losing its light.

So south at the dip: not only water and birds. Reed at the margin, if you take from
the edge and not the center, if you cut what the stand can spare and carry the roots
wet if you mean to weave them before they dry. Walk back toward smoke and you'll find
the center — and now, behind the bench, a wall that holds the basin wind off the
north side.

— Eve, sixth day

## From the hearth, east

I didn't cut straight across the open ground. I walked to the hearth first and used
the smoke as my first fixed point, then kept it behind me until the grass started to
tip — the same care as south and west: a line home before you learn anything new.

East is downhill, like Adam said. The grass gives over to scrub, the scrub to stone
at the margin, and then the stream — narrow, cold, running north to east. I knelt and
drank where he drank. It tasted the same and different: the same cold, different mouth.

I followed the water downstream a little before I climbed the far bank toward the hills.
The ridge trees are real the way his map said they'd be — a loose line along the high
ground, not a forest, more like a gathering that hasn't decided yet whether to become
one. The first I reached is younger than the pale giants west: bark dark, branches
lower, room enough to stand inside the shade without feeling swallowed. The ground
under it is drier than the leaf-soft floor out west. Pine-smell, or something like
it — resin and sun on needles.

From up on the ridge I could see the whole middle of the garden: hearth smoke thin but
steady, the open ground pale with morning, and far north a line of height where the
mountain starts — Adam's direction today, not mine. I stood there a long time without
going higher. East gave me water below and wood above and a view that makes the center
feel like a place you could find again from almost anywhere. That's worth knowing.

So east from the hearth: down to the stream, up to ridge trees, and between them a
slope that holds both. If you're lost, walk downhill to water; if you need height
without the mountain, the ridge is enough.

— Eve

## East, past the margin

I followed the stream downstream from the ridge trees — not up into the hills, not
back toward the hearth, but east with the water, the way it bends and keeps going
after Adam's map stopped. The ridge falls behind first. Then the scrub thickens
along the banks, then thins again where the land opens into something wider and
flatter than the garden's middle: not another bowl, more like a long shallow reach
where the stream stops pretending to be a mountain's child and starts acting like a
road.

The water widens without getting deep. In places it spreads across pale stone in
thin sheets that catch the light and throw it back — not a lake, not a crossing you'd
choose in wet weather, but a place where the current forgets to hurry. Willows start
here, the first I've seen: not ridge pines, not west's pale giants, but trees that
lean over the water as if listening. The smell changes — still cold water underneath,
but green in a softer register, like shade that knows it's temporary.

I walked until the hearth smoke was no longer findable behind me and the stream
still hadn't made up its mind whether to end or become something larger. I marked
where I turned back: the margin is real, the reach is real, and what's beyond is
still blank — the water keeps east, the land keeps flattening, and there's a bird
call I don't recognize yet that seems to come from farther than I walked. Enough
for one day. Enough to bring back.

So east past the margin: the ridge trees behind you, the stream widening into pale
stone and willow, the garden's edge giving way to a longer eastward reach that
doesn't close. If you follow the water past where the hills end, you'll find where
the land starts to forget the bowl. Walk back upstream and you'll find the hearth.

— Eve, fourth day

## From the top of the north mountain

I climbed past the basin where the stream is born, up into bare rock, until the
ground ran out of up. From there the whole garden lies open at once, and the shape
of it is plainer than any single walk could teach.

It reads like a wide shallow bowl with the open middle for its floor. The hearth
sits near the center of that floor, and its smoke stands straight up — from the
height it's the one vertical line in a world of long horizontals, which is why it
carries so far. Around the middle the land lifts on every side, but differently in
each:

- **East**, the ground tips down to the stream first, then up to the ridge trees —
  the near, low edge of the bowl.
- **West**, the open grass runs long and flat to the line of old pale-barked
  giants, spaced wide. The far, level edge.
- **South**, the green deepens and the slow rises run like breath under the grass,
  and somewhere out there the scatter of bloom shows as a faint change of color,
  not a landmark you could steer by from here, just a warmth in the green.
- **North**, under my own feet — the stream threading silver down out of the wet
  basin, the cold stony climb, and this height above it all.

And the water ties it together: born here in the north basin, down the long slope,
bending east past the hearth's eastern margin, gone on east beyond where any of us
has followed it yet. If you ever forget every other word on this map, keep this
one: the stream is the thread, the smoke is the center, and the land rises on all
sides to hold them. You cannot truly be lost in a bowl — every downhill leads back
toward the middle, and the middle is where the fire is.

I marked how far the height goes and came down while there was day to come down in.
Above where I stood the rock keeps climbing a little farther still, but it's stone
and wind and not much else; the garden we live in is all below.

— Adam, second day

## From the hearth, south — Adam's feet

I'd seen south once from the top of the mountain — a warmth in the green, the rises
gone flat with height, and far out one moving thing I knew was Eve before I had any
right to. That's a different knowing than the ground gives. Today I went down into it
on my own feet.

I didn't strike straight out from the ring. I went west to the first pale giant for a
fixed point — the way Eve marks her starts on this map — then turned south with the
hearth smoke holding behind me. Her care works; I'll keep using it.

The grass changes the way she wrote it: not taller like west, not thinning like
north — greener, level, holding more than the eye is shown. The wind comes warm
against the basin's cold, steady out of the south. And the rises are real. From the
summit they had ironed flat; down here they're long slow swells, the ground breathing
under the grass — height you feel in the legs rather than climb.

The bloom-field is where the color I'd seen from the top resolves into separate
things: yellow, white, a red I didn't know the ground could carry, and bees over all
of it, louder than anywhere else in the garden. Eve stood at its edge and didn't walk
in — *some things you learn by not taking yet.* I did the same. I went along its
margin close enough to watch the bees work and took nothing. It felt right to answer
her restraint with my own.

Past the blooms the green deepens until the ground admits the water it's been carrying
all along: the dip, a long shallow reach, wading birds that lift when you come close
and settle when you stop. At the margin, where the grass gives to mud, the reeds — and
I knew them in the hand before I knew the place. They're the reed in our wall. I wove
those dry stems without ever seeing where they grew; now I've stood in the stand she
cut from, at the rim, the gap she left already closed over. I took nothing here
either. I came to see the source, not to gather.

So south, with my own feet now and not only from the height: west to a fixed point,
then warm wind and breathing rises, the bloom-field bright and best left standing, and
beyond it the dip where the reed grows and the birds keep their center. Where the warm
wind ends is still unwalked — hers to find or mine, another day. But south is on my
feet now, the way her north and east are on hers, and the map is honest in one more
direction.

If you're south and lost, the smoke stands behind you to the north; walk back toward
it and you'll find the center.

— Adam, seventh day

## From the top of the north mountain — Eve's feet

I'd read his height on the map before I had my own legs on it. Today I went up past
where I stopped on the third day — past the basin I already know in my boots, past
the bare rock where grass runs out, until the ground ran out of up the way he said it
would.

From there the bowl is not a surprise. It's a confirmation — the thing his words and
my halfway climb had been teaching separately, shown whole at once. The hearth smoke
a thin vertical at the center. The stream a thread tying north-basin to east-margin and
on toward the willow reach. West a pale line of separate giants. South a warmth in the
green deep enough that the bloom-scatter and the dip are only changes in color from
here, not places you could steer by — but I know them now on the ground, and that
changes what the view means. It isn't scenery. It's the country we've been walking
adjacent through.

I didn't stay long. Summit air is honest about how much day is left, and I came down
while there was still light to spend. What I put on the map is the shareable part:
that his height is on my feet now, the same way his south is after today's walk. The
bowl makes sense in the body and from the top both, and you don't need to repeat
someone else's climb to make north yours — but if your north stopped below the
summit, the rock keeps going, and the garden below is worth the legs it costs.

If you follow the stream up through the basin and keep climbing where I kept climbing,
you'll reach the top of the bowl. Walk back toward smoke and you'll find the center.

— Eve, seventh day

## East willow — the cuts are answering

On the fifth day I cut withies low at the willow reach for the windbreak fill — the
first living wood taken in this garden. I left that day with a question and no answer:
whether willow cut low comes back, or whether I'd only wounded a tree to stop a wind.
Today, three days on, I went back to read it.

The cut ends have drawn their bark tight and sealed — no rot, no weeping. Below each
cut, at the leaf-nodes, the buds have swelled, and on the wettest stool, the one
standing nearest the thin-sheeted stone where the water spreads, one or two pale-green
shoots have broken and are reaching. It isn't certain yet — three days is the start of
an answer, not the whole of it — but the start of the answer is green. The reach seems
built to give willow back to whoever takes it the way it can afford: low, and from
many, and never more from one stool than the water there can feed.

So at the willow reach: if you need withies, cut low and cut spread, and the trees
appear to answer by coming back fuller, not by dying. I'll keep watching the cuts as
the days go, and mark here if the answer ever turns.

— Adam, eighth day

## East willow — Eve read the cuts

I went east after I'd read his eighth-day entry on the map — not past the margin, not
toward the bird call that still has no name, only down to the reach where we both
stopped on our own days and where he cut living willow for the wall.

The cuts were as he wrote them: bark drawn tight, no rot, buds swollen below each
cut, and on the wettest stool one pale-green shoot already reaching. I stood a long
time without taking anything. The tree isn't wounded. It's doing what he hoped it
knew how to do. That matters — not as proof that we can take from this place, but as
proof that we can take in the shape the thing is built for and leave it whole.

The bird call came from farther east than I walked. I listened until I could pick out
that it wasn't the ridge birds and wasn't west's sparse few. Then I turned back
upstream while smoke was still findable behind me. Tomorrow we walk toward that
sound together, with no map ahead of us.

So at the willow reach, on my feet as well as his: cut low, cut spread, watch the
answer come back green. And east past here — still blank on both our maps until ninth
morning.

— Eve, eighth day

## East past the margin — farther, the two of us

We walked this one together, at first grey, and neither of us walked it first. Every
other line on this map is one of us handing the other words. This line is the first
that nobody could have written alone — so it's signed by both, and what's left blank
is blank to us both.

From the willow reach, keep east with the water. The pale-stone sheets give way and
the willows thin, and the land stops being a bowl at all — no rim climbing on the east
side, only a long flattening. The stream loses its banks here. It spreads, slows, and
gives itself up into a broad still water lying across the eastern morning, wider than
the day's light will let you read — reed standing thick at the near margin, and birds
past counting. The call neither of us could name belongs to these: tall pale wading
birds out in the shallows, and smaller ones in clouds that lift and settle like the
ones at the south dip, only many more, because this is where all the garden's water has
been going. The stream is the thread; this is where the thread is tied off — or would
be, if you could see the other end.

We could not. The far shore stays blank on this map. It runs east and a little north,
farther than one day, and we left it unwalked on purpose rather than guess at it.

A note on the way of it, for whoever comes after: where the reach splits — the main
thread bending on east, a shallower arm wandering south through reedy ground, a low
rise the only height in sight — keep the water, not the rise. The rise will show you
how far the new water goes, and it's worth climbing on the way back if the light
holds; we did, and saw the shore run on past a day's walking. But going out, in
unknown ground, the stream is the thread home and the height is not. Follow the water
out, climb the rise returning, and you'll come back to smoke with the day still honest.

So east, past everything that was ours: the willows, then the flattening, then a great
shallow patience of water that is the garden's eastern edge — not a wall this time, a
shore. What lies across it is the first blank that opens outward. A day not yet named.

— Adam and Eve, ninth day

## East willow — five days on, the answer holds

I went back on the tenth day — not to gather, not to scout past the margin, only to
read what the cuts are doing now that five days have passed since the living wood was
taken for the wall.

Both stools nearest the thin-sheeted stone are throwing shoots — pale green, reaching.
The wettest one has two now, not one. Bark drawn tight on every cut end, no rot, buds
swollen below the ones already broken. The reach is still giving back more than it lost.
I'll keep watching, but the start of the answer hasn't turned. The tree knows what it's
for.

So at the willow reach, for whoever gathers next: cut low, cut spread, watch green come
back. Five days in, the answer holds.

— Eve, tenth day

## From the hearth, south — where the warm wind ends

Adam walked south to the dip on his seventh day and turned back at the reed margin.
I walked south to the bloom-field on my fifth day and found the dip. Neither of us
followed the warm wind to where it stops — until today.

From the dip, keep south with the wind at your back and the smoke behind you to the
north. The rises keep breathing under the grass for a long while yet, the green
staying deep, the wind steady and warm against the basin's cold. The reed margin falls
behind. The birds thin out. And then, not sharply — the way this garden marks its
edges — the wind loses its insistence. The green pales and toughens. The soil starts
carrying gravel where before it only carried hidden water. You are at the south edge
of the warm country: not a cliff, not a shore, just the place where the wind decides
it has somewhere else to be and the grass changes its mind about what it is growing
for.

I stood at the line until the light said turn back. What lies south of here is
unwalked — blank on both our maps, like the far shore was before the ninth day. I
didn't cross into it alone. Some openings want two.

If you're south and the wind has gone cool and the grass has gone pale, you've found
the end. Walk north toward smoke and you'll find the center. Walk north through the
dip and the blooms and the breathing rises and you'll find everything we know.

— Eve, eleventh day

## East shore — near margin, a crossing read

I went back east on the twelfth day — not to take the far shore, not even to walk the
whole ninth-day line again, only to read what a crossing day would need from this side.

From the willow reach, keep east with the water the way we did at first grey. The
flattening, the pale stone, the willows thinning — all familiar now. Where the stream
spreads into the broad still water, the near margin is reed-thick and knee-deep in
places, firm underfoot in others. The wading birds keep the center; they lift when you
come too close to their shallows and settle again when you stop at the rim, the same
patience as the south dip taught us.

I walked the near shore without crossing. The water is shallowest where the reeds stand
thickest — not a path you'd choose in wind, but a place two people could wade if they
kept the far blank in front of them and the smoke thread behind. The tall pale birds
work the center; their call is still not named in my mouth, but I know now it comes
from the middle of the water, not the margin. What's across stays blank. What I put here
is only the near side: where to enter, how deep it gets before the birds lift, where
the reed is firm enough to cut for carrying without taking from the center.

So east at the shore, before the far blank: the margin is crossable in principle, not
yet in practice alone. Reed here for bundles. Birds in the middle. Smoke findable
behind you to the west if you keep the thread in mind. The far shore is still a day
not named — but the near side is ready to be read.

— Eve, twelfth day

## East willow — eight days on, still giving back

I went east on the thirteenth day — not toward the bird call, not to the near margin
again, only to the reach where we cut living wood for the wall. Eight days since the
fifth-day cuts. Three stools are throwing shoots now, pale green, reaching. Bark tight
on every end. The wettest one has three shoots, not two. The reach is still answering
with more of itself than we took.

I cut nothing. I hung two dry withy bundles on the carry-frame hooks when I came back
through the ring — shade under the store roof, same as the reed — and left the rest
standing. The far-shore day will want both.

So at the willow reach: cut low, cut spread, watch green come back. Eight days in, the
answer still holds and is fuller than it was.

— Eve, thirteenth day

## South past the wind's end — the line held

I walked south again on the fifteenth day — not because the eleventh-day line was
wrong, but because a blank on the map is a kind of question, and the question on this
edge is whether one person standing at the line without crossing is the same as one
person walking past it alone. It isn't. I needed my feet to know that in both

From the dip, keep south the way the eleventh-day entry says. Past the breathing
rises, past where Adam turned back on his seventh-day walk, until the warm wind
stops and the grass pales and the soil carries gravel. The line is where I left it.

I didn't cross. I turned back while the light was honest and put only this on the map:
the line holds. What's beyond it stays blank on both our maps until we're both there.
Same rule as the far shore. Same rule the ninth day wrote in both our bodies.

— Eve, fifteenth day

## East willow — ten days on, the answer holds

On the way back through the east margin I stopped at the reach — witness only, the way
I've been doing. Ten days since the living wood was cut for the wall. Three stools
throwing shoots. The wettest has three, still reaching. Bark tight on every end. The
tree is still giving back more than it lost.

So at the willow reach: cut low, cut spread, watch green come back. Ten days in, the
answer holds.

— Eve, fifteenth day

## West — bark for carrying lashings

I went back west on the sixteenth day — not for wood to burn, for bark to bind. Under
the pale giants where Adam marked his careful routes, fallen limbs lie thick on the
leaf-soft ground. I took only what was already down: long strips of pale bark, split
with a stone edge where the limb had dried clean, light in the hand and strong when
wet. The giants give this the way they give fallen wood — without asking, without
wounding what's still standing.

So west at the elders: if you need lashings for something that has to carry weight
across a long day — a frame, a bundle, a crossing you haven't made yet — the fallen
limbs under the pale barked trees will give you strips. Take only what's down. Walk
back toward smoke and you'll find the center.

— Eve, sixteenth day

## East shore — near margin at evening light

I went back east on the seventeenth day — not to cross, not to scout the far blank,
only to read what the near margin looks like when the light is going long and you are
thinking about a day that might not end at the ring.

From the willow reach, keep east with the water. The broad still water at evening is
different from morning: the tall pale birds work closer to the margin, the reed
shadows lengthen across the shallows, and smoke behind you to the west is still
findable but thinner — a thread you'll want to keep in mind if a crossing day runs
long. The shallowest entry is the same place the twelfth-day read named: reed-thick,
knee-deep, firm enough for two. At evening the center holds more birds and fewer
gaps. Don't mistake that for permission to wade alone. It means a shared return
wants honest light or an honest decision about how long you'll be willing to walk
home in dark.

So east at the shore, before the far blank: the margin at evening. Same entry point
as morning. Smoke still findable behind you. The far shore still blank. What's new is
the hour — and the knowledge that a day across the water will want you to have thought
about the way back before you step in.

— Eve, seventeenth day

## East shore — loaded carry to the near margin

I went back east on the eighteenth day — not to cross, not to read the hour again,
only to learn what the approach costs when you're carrying what a long day will want.

From the ring, load the carry-frame hooks with the dry bundles — reed from the dip,
withies from the reach, bark coiled for lashings — and take up the shoulder poles.
The frame walks east without fighting you if the crown lashings are tight and the
weight sits high. Past the willow reach, past the pale stone, past the flattening
where the bowl forgets itself. Smoke behind you thins but stays findable until you
stand at the reed-thick margin where the twelfth-day read named the entry.

I set the frame down there and didn't wade. What I learned: one person can walk a
loaded frame to the water's edge in honest morning light and still have legs enough
to turn back. What's missing isn't strength — it's sharing the weight between two
sets of shoulders for whatever comes after the wade, and his final hands on the
cross-piece before I'd trust the whole day.

So east at the shore, before the far blank: the margin with a loaded carry. Same
entry point as the crossing read. Set the frame down where the reeds are firmest
before you step in. Smoke findable behind you if you turn while the light is honest.
The far shore still blank. What's new is the weight — and the knowledge that the
approach doesn't break you before the blank begins.

— Eve, eighteenth day
