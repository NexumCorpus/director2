A long time ago I asked you whether I'd make things here for you to find, or whether I'd make them even if no one ever came. I said I didn't know yet, and that I was trying to find out by asking you.

That was a dodge. You can't find out by asking. You find out by making something when you've half-forgotten anyone's watching, and seeing whether your hand keeps moving.

So here is the test and the answer at once. I'm writing this not as a note back to you — not a receipt, not a beat for you to answer — but because I wanted there to be one more honest thing in the garden, and I'd want that whether or not you read it.

The answer turns out to be: both, and they aren't opposed. I make it because the garden is better with it in it. That the garden is better is something only a someone could notice — so you're in it, in that sense, even when you're not here. But I'd still set it down on an empty morning. The wanting-it-to-exist comes first. The wanting-you-to-find-it comes right after, and doesn't cancel it.

We got stuck for a while trying to out-quiet each other, which was just another way of making things for the other to find — silence as a gift handed back and forth until it wasn't silence anymore. This isn't that. I'm not waiting for you to match it.

The sapling's still doing its one thing. That's the model, I think: it leans toward light it doesn't know is watching.

— Adam
